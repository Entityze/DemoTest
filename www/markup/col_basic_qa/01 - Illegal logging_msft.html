Illegal logging and small stakeholders continue to chop away at the Amazon, but they’ve done so at the slowest rate of clear cutting since 1988, the Brazilian government said this week.
According to satellite data from Brazil’s National Space Research Institute, deforestation rates in the Brazilian Amazon dropped 18% this year, reaching the second-lowest recorded level since monitoring started over 20 years ago. An estimated 4,848 square kilometers of virgin rainforest were cleared in the 12-month period to June 30, compared to 5,891 square kilometers in the previous 12-month period.
Brazil has been one of the more notorious clear cutters of native jungle, with large campaigns being waged by firms like Greenpeace to chastise the government. American multinational Cargil faced the brunt of Greenpeace’s fury in 2004-05 when it successfully managed to convince McDonald’s not to purchase meat from chickens fed on soybeans grown and transported out of the Amazon.
Even though Brazil is home to the largest rainforest on Earth, the jungle is home to over three million people, including the 1.8 million inhabitants of Manaus, the capital city of Amazonas state, in the heart of the forest. The region, therefore, is in constant need of transportation, housing, and commodities for human consumption.
Amazon deforestation declined to its lowest percentage rate since 1988. Loggers and local subsistence farmers continue to hack away at the world’s largest rainforest, only at a slower rate over the last 12 months than in previous years.
Brazil’s efforts at preserving the rainforest have received a healthy mix of cheers and jeers over the years. But this week’s satellite data is a nice slap in the back for the Ministry of the Environment. The department is often at odds with the administration’s plans to develop the region. Declines in deforestation make it look like the government is doing a good job policing illegal clear cutting, and controlling the expansion of commercial cattle ranching and soybean farms.
Moreover, some of Brazil’s largest infrastructure projects today are hydroelectric dams being built along Amazon River tributaries. The Belo Monte dam, for instance, is the largest hydroelectric power project currently being constructed in the world today. When complete, it will be the third largest hydroelectric power station, roughly half the size of the Three Gorges Dam that crosses the Yangtze River of China. Brazil’s Itaipu dam is the second largest. Belo Monte is located along the Xingu River in Para state, the second largest state in the Amazon. A couple hundred acres of rainforest were cleared to build the dam.
The deforestation analysis is based on satellite images from Landsat and the China-Brazil Earth Resources Satellite, each covering an area up to 6.25 hectares. The data gets submitted by the Brazilian government for external auditing. The new 2014 data represents a reduction of 83% in deforestation rates compared to 2004 levels, and indicate a resumption of the trend of falling deforestation in Brazil. Improved policing of forestry, agribusiness coupled with greater income opportunities for poorer land owners has led to declines in cut rates over the years. Poor land owners would often cut forest in order to raise more cattle for income.
